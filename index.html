Commas are covered in depth elsewhere in this module. This page focuses on the four marks that secondary students most consistently misuse or avoid entirely: the semicolon, the colon, the em dash, and the hyphen. These marks are not interchangeable with each other or with the comma—each has a specific structural function, and using the wrong one (or avoiding all of them) limits the range of sentence structures available to a writer.

Students often avoid these marks because they're uncertain about the rules. The goal of this page is to make those rules concrete enough that students can use each mark deliberately—and to show how each one creates effects that no other mark can replicate.

The Semicolon

Semicolons — Joining and Separating

A semicolon has two uses: joining two closely related independent clauses without a conjunction, and separating items in a complex list where the items themselves contain commas. It is stronger than a comma and weaker than a period.

Use 1 — Joining independent clauses

A semicolon between two independent clauses signals that the ideas are closely related—more so than a period would suggest—but without naming the relationship the way a conjunction would. Use it when the connection is so clear it doesn't need to be stated.

✓ "AI tools can generate grammatically fluent text; they cannot verify whether that text is accurate."

✓ "Cognitive offloading reduces short-term effort; it may also reduce long-term skill development."

When a conjunctive adverb (however, therefore, thus, consequently, moreover, nevertheless, furthermore) follows the semicolon, the adverb is followed by a comma:

✓ "The output sounded authoritative; however, it contained three factual errors."
✓ "AI use is increasing among students; therefore, academic integrity policies need to be updated."

✕ Common errors

"AI tools are useful; but they require critical evaluation." — Don't use a semicolon before a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS). Use a comma instead: "AI tools are useful, but they require critical evaluation."

"AI tools are useful; requiring critical evaluation from users." — A semicolon must be followed by an independent clause, not a phrase. The second element here is a participial phrase.

Use 2 — Separating complex list items

When the items in a series themselves contain commas, using commas between items creates confusion about where one item ends and the next begins. A semicolon between items makes the boundaries clear.

✓ "The study included participants from Austin, Texas; Portland, Oregon; and Burlington, Vermont."

✓ "The researchers examined three models: GPT-4, which was trained on internet text; Gemini, which was trained on a proprietary dataset; and Claude, which was trained with a focus on constitutional alignment."

Teaching tip: Students often avoid semicolons because they're unsure of the rule. The clearest single test: can you replace the semicolon with a period and have two complete sentences? If yes, the semicolon is probably correct (assuming no FANBOYS follows it). If no, something else is needed. The period test is faster and more reliable than asking students to diagram the sentence.

The Colon

Colons — Introducing and Announcing

A colon introduces what follows—a list, a quotation, an explanation, or an appositive—by signaling that what comes after it completes, illustrates, or elaborates on what came before. The key rule: what comes before the colon must be a complete independent clause.

Use 1 — Introducing a list

✓ "Critical AI literacy involves three core competencies: source verification, output evaluation, and bias recognition."

✓ "Gerlich's study found three significant patterns: higher AI use correlated with lower critical thinking scores, younger participants showed greater AI dependence, and higher educational attainment moderated the effect."

Use 2 — Introducing an explanation or elaboration

The colon can replace a phrase like that is, in other words, or specifically to introduce an explanation of the preceding clause.

✓ "Chiang's argument has a counterintuitive implication: the more fluent an AI output sounds, the less detectable its factual errors are."

✓ "The blurry JPEG metaphor captures something specific about hallucinations: they are not random errors but artifacts of compression—statistically plausible reconstructions of missing information."

Use 3 — Introducing a formal quotation

✓ "Roe et al. define Critical AI Literacy precisely: 'the ability to critically analyse and engage with AI systems by understanding their technical foundations, societal implications, and embedded power structures.'"

✕ Common errors

"The three competencies are: source verification, output evaluation, and bias recognition." — The colon interrupts a linking verb and its complement. Remove the colon: "The three competencies are source verification, output evaluation, and bias recognition."

"Students need to: verify sources, evaluate outputs, and recognize bias." — A colon cannot follow an infinitive. Remove it or restructure: "Students need to do three things: verify sources, evaluate outputs, and recognize bias."

Capitalization after a colon

Style guides differ slightly here. APA and Chicago capitalize the first word after a colon if what follows is a complete independent clause. MLA does not capitalize unless the word is a proper noun. In secondary ELA, either convention is acceptable if applied consistently—what matters is that students know a convention exists and apply it deliberately.

Teaching tip: The simplest way to teach the colon is the "complete sentence before" rule: cover everything after the colon and read what remains. Does it form a complete sentence? If yes, the colon is probably correct. If no—if there's a linking verb with no complement, or an infinitive with no object—the colon needs to go. This test catches the two most common colon errors immediately.

The Em Dash

Em Dashes — Interrupting and Emphasizing

The em dash (—) is the longest of the three dashes. It is more emphatic than a comma and more informal than a colon or parentheses. It signals an interruption, a sharp pivot, or a dramatic elaboration. Used sparingly, it creates energy. Used constantly, it becomes a crutch that replaces the structural thinking that a colon or comma would require.

Use 1 — Setting off an interruption or appositive (in place of commas or parentheses)

Like commas and parentheses, dashes can set off nonessential material. The choice among the three signals different degrees of emphasis: commas are neutral, parentheses de-emphasize, dashes emphasize.

✓ "Chiang's argument—that LLMs are lossy compression algorithms rather than reasoning systems—has significant implications for writing instruction."

✓ "The output sounded confident—disturbingly so—but contained three factual errors."

Use 2 — Introducing a list or explanation with added emphasis

Similar to the colon, but more dramatic. Use when the introduction is already emphatic or when the list is itself being foregrounded.

✓ "Three things make an AI output dangerous—fluency, confidence, and plausibility—because they are the qualities readers use as proxies for accuracy."

✓ "The model did what LLMs always do—it interpolated."

Use 3 — Marking an abrupt interruption or shift

✓ "The student submitted the essay—then discovered it had been generated entirely by AI."

✓ "It was fluent, polished, convincingly organized—and completely fabricated."

Forming the em dash

In most word processors, two hyphens (--) typed without spaces will autocorrect to an em dash. In HTML, it is the entity &mdash; or the Unicode character —. In typed and handwritten work, two hyphens with no spaces on either side are the standard substitute.

Style guides differ on spacing: AP style uses spaces on both sides of the em dash; Chicago and MLA use no spaces. Follow whichever style guide your course or school uses, and apply it consistently.

✕ Overuse

The em dash loses its punch when used in every sentence. If a student's draft has more than one or two em dashes per paragraph, ask them to replace each one with the mark that best fits the structural function—comma, colon, parentheses, or semicolon—and reserve the dash for moments where the interruption or emphasis genuinely earns it.

Teaching tip: The em dash is the most rhetorically interesting mark in this set, and students who discover it often overuse it for a period before calibrating. That's fine—let the overcorrection happen, then teach the calibration. A student who overuses em dashes has understood that punctuation does rhetorical work; that's the harder lesson. The specific usage rules are easier to refine once the underlying insight is there.

The Hyphen

Hyphens — Compounding and Dividing

The hyphen (-) is the shortest of the three dash-family marks and serves entirely different functions from the em dash. It connects parts of compound words and modifiers; it does not interrupt or set off elements the way a dash does. The rules for hyphens are more fluid than for the other marks—conventions vary by style guide and evolve over time—but a few reliable patterns cover most cases students encounter.

Use 1 — Compound modifiers before a noun

When two or more words function together as a single modifier before a noun, hyphenate them. When the same words come after the noun, they usually don't need a hyphen.

✓ "an AI-generated essay" — hyphenated before the noun
✓ "an essay that was AI generated" — no hyphen after the noun
✓ "a well-documented problem" → "a problem that is well documented"
✓ "a large-scale study" → "a study conducted on a large scale"

Use 2 — Compound nouns

Many compound nouns are hyphenated; many others are written as one word or two. Usage evolves—when in doubt, check a current dictionary for the standard form.

Hyphenated: self-regulation, follow-up, check-in, sign-in, trade-off
One word: proofreading, overreliance, dataset, chatbot, feedback
Two words: critical thinking, writing process, source verification

Use 3 — Prefixes

Most prefixes attach directly without a hyphen (overreliance, preservice, nonstandardized, multiliterate). Hyphens are used with prefixes when the prefix precedes a proper noun or numeral (pre-AI, non-Western, post-1990), or when the unhyphenated form would cause confusion (re-cover vs. recover, re-create vs. recreate).

Use 4 — Numbers and fractions

✓ "twenty-one through ninety-nine when spelled out"
✓ "a two-thirds majority" — fraction used as modifier
✓ "two thirds of the outputs" — fraction used as noun phrase, no hyphen

Common errors

✕ "an AI generated essay" — missing hyphen in compound modifier before noun
✕ "a high school teacher's well-documented concerns" — high school is a compound noun, not a compound modifier here; no hyphen needed
✕ "self regulation" — established compound noun requires a hyphen

Teaching tip: The compound modifier rule (hyphenate before the noun, not after) is the one hyphen rule worth teaching explicitly at the secondary level—it covers the majority of hyphen decisions students face in academic writing. The others are best handled as needed when they arise in student drafts, since dictionary conventions change and no single rule covers all cases.

Quick Reference — Choosing Among the Four Marks

These marks are not interchangeable. Each has a specific job. When you're unsure which to use, ask which function you need:

Semicolon ;

  • Join two closely related independent clauses without a conjunction
  • Separate complex list items that contain internal commas
  • Never before a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS)
  • Never before a phrase (only before an independent clause)

Colon :

  • Introduce a list, explanation, or quotation
  • Must follow a complete independent clause
  • Never after a linking verb (are:) or an infinitive (to:)
  • More formal than a dash; less emphatic

Em Dash —

  • Set off interruptions, appositives, or asides with emphasis
  • Introduce a list or explanation dramatically
  • Mark an abrupt shift or interruption
  • Use sparingly; overuse drains its rhetorical power

Hyphen -

  • Join compound modifiers before a noun
  • Form established compound nouns and words
  • Attach certain prefixes to proper nouns or to avoid ambiguity
  • Write out numbers 21–99 and spelled-out fractions used as modifiers

Self-Check: Identify and Correct

Each item contains an error involving one of the four marks covered on this page. Identify the error and correct it.

Item 1

"The study had three major limitations; small sample size, limited geographic diversity, and a short data collection window."

Show answer

Error: A semicolon is used where a colon is needed. The semicolon must be followed by an independent clause. What follows here is a list—the correct mark to introduce a list from a complete independent clause is a colon.

Revision: "The study had three major limitations: small sample size, limited geographic diversity, and a short data collection window."

Item 2

"AI generated text can be: fluent, confident, and factually wrong at the same time."

Show answer

Errors: Two errors. First, the colon interrupts a linking verb and its complements—the colon cannot follow can be. Second, AI generated is a compound modifier before a noun and needs a hyphen.

Revision: "AI-generated text can be fluent, confident, and factually wrong at the same time."

If a colon is desired for emphasis: "AI-generated text can achieve something paradoxical: sounding fluent, confident, and factually wrong simultaneously."

Item 3

"The model performed well on structured tasks; but struggled significantly with open-ended reasoning questions."

Show answer

Error: A semicolon before a coordinating conjunction (but). A semicolon must be followed by an independent clause; what follows here is not independent—but struggled significantly has no subject. The semicolon should be a comma.

Revision: "The model performed well on structured tasks, but struggled significantly with open-ended reasoning questions."

Note: Alternatively, this could be rewritten as a compound sentence: "The model performed well on structured tasks, but it struggled significantly with open-ended reasoning questions."

Item 4

"Chiang's metaphor—that LLMs are blurry JPEGs of the web captures something precise about hallucinations—they are not random errors but artifacts of compression."

Show answer

Error: The first em dash opens a parenthetical but is not closed before the second dash begins a new function (introducing an elaboration). The sentence has two dashes but only one opening dash where two are needed for the parenthetical, with the elaboration handled separately.

Revision A (paired dashes for the appositive, colon for the elaboration): "Chiang's metaphor—that LLMs are blurry JPEGs of the web—captures something precise about hallucinations: they are not random errors but artifacts of compression."

Revision B (recast to avoid ambiguity): "Chiang's metaphor captures something precise about hallucinations. In describing LLMs as blurry JPEGs of the web, he implies that hallucinations are not random errors but artifacts of compression."

Item 5

"The researchers studied participants from three cities: Austin, Texas, Portland, Oregon, and Burlington, Vermont, and found that AI use correlated with lower self reported critical thinking scores."

Show answer

Errors: Two errors. First, the city-state list uses commas both between list items and between city and state, making the list boundaries invisible—semicolons are needed between items. Second, self reported is a compound modifier before a noun and needs a hyphen.

Revision: "The researchers studied participants from three cities: Austin, Texas; Portland, Oregon; and Burlington, Vermont, and found that AI use correlated with lower self-reported critical thinking scores."
